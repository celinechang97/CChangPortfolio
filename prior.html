How might we help college students
prioritize
their tasks better
so that they become more
efficient?

My teammates and I created a web application to help college students organize and prioritize their tasks in daily, weekly, and monthly views. This is different from a normal calendar in that it is targeted to help people get things done. This platform guides users by breaking down their progress - it shows when they plan on completing the task, how long it will take them to complete it, the importance of the task, and they can also mark it as done when they have accomplished it. As seen below, each card holds all the necessary information mentioned above, with colors representing the importance to help users visualize priority – the darker the shade of red, the higher the priority of the task.

how we started
Students lead busy lives – balancing between school, work, extracurricular and social activities. With such a busy schedule, it is critical that they stay on top of their responsibilities. However, it can be tough to know where to start.
insights from interviews & observations
Students’ biggest pain of their current modes of organization was
difficult accessibility and usage.
They spent too much time organizing the tasks than actually executing them as it didn't actually help them prioritize all the tasks. Moreover, many people used multiple methods of organization to keep track of all the different tasks in their lives, which was inefficient as they had to navigate back and forth between their applications.
prototyping, user-testing, & iterating
Throughout the ten week process of this project, we conducted user-testing with at least three people every week. We started out with a paper prototype to ensure that all of the necessary tasks were clear. Some of the takeaways from this stage was that our original way of indicating the importance of the task was confusing and that people found the tasks too spread out. We then mocked up a rough version on the computer to ensure that the flow of the application was intuitive. From the testings, we found that people didn't want to manually type in how many minutes it was going to take them to complete the task, that the filter buttons were confusing without a label, and that people wanted more choices other than "social, work, and class."
